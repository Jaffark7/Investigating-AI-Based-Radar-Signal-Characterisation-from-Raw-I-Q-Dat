# Radar AI Project Plan

## What This Project Is

This is a personal learning project inspired by a real thesis posting from Saab, the Swedish defence company. The original idea was to investigate whether AI can detect and characterise radar targets directly from raw I/Q data, instead of using the traditional step-by-step signal processing pipeline.

We are building our own version of that idea — independently, with open public data, and with the goal of learning deeply while producing something real and impressive enough to show as a portfolio project.

---

## The Core Idea in Plain English

A radar works by sending out a pulse of radio energy and listening for the echo that bounces back from objects (planes, drones, cars etc). The raw recording of that echo is called I/Q data — two streams of numbers (I and Q) that together fully describe the returning signal at every moment in time.

Traditionally, engineers process this raw I/Q through a chain of mathematical steps to extract useful information: where is the target, how far away, how fast is it moving. Each step adds delay and can lose some information.

The question this project explores is: **can a neural network learn to extract that same information directly from the raw I/Q, skipping most of those steps?**

To answer that properly, we need to:
- Build the traditional method ourselves (so we understand it and can compare against it)
- Train a neural network on the same data
- Compare both under different conditions, especially noisy ones

---

## The Dataset

We are using **RadChar**, a free and publicly available radar I/Q dataset published by researchers at IEEE ICASSP. It is synthetic (generated by simulation, not a real radar) but realistic, well-labelled, and specifically designed for machine learning on radar signals.

Each sample in RadChar is a raw I/Q recording of a radar waveform, with labels that tell you the signal type and parameters. This means we have ground truth — we know the right answer for every sample, which is exactly what we need for supervised learning and for evaluating both the traditional and AI approaches.

RadChar GitHub: https://github.com/abcxyzi/RadChar

## The Six Steps

### Step 1 — Get a Feel for Radar
Watch a small number of videos to understand what radar is, what a range-Doppler map looks like, and what I/Q data represents conceptually. The goal is not to become an expert. The goal is to not be lost when looking at real data in Step 2.

**Why:** You cannot build something you do not understand at a basic level. This step costs very little time and pays off in every step after it.

---

### Step 2 — Download RadChar and Explore the Data
Download the RadChar dataset. Open it in a Jupyter notebook. Plot the I component and Q component of a few samples. Look at what different signal types look like. Understand what the labels mean.

**Why:** Before building anything, you need to know your data. What does a sample look like? How long is it? What are we predicting? What does noise look like in this data? Answering these questions first prevents wasted effort later.

---

### Step 3 — Build the Traditional (Conventional) Detector
Implement matched filtering and a basic threshold detector on the RadChar data. This is the classic engineering approach to finding targets in radar signals — no AI involved, just maths.

**Why:** This is the baseline. Without it, your neural network results have no context. "My model gets 90% accuracy" is meaningless. "My model gets 90% and the traditional method gets 74% on the same data, especially at low signal-to-noise ratios" is a finding. The comparison is the heart of the project. You also learn the most about radar DSP in this step.

---

### Step 4 — Train the Neural Network
Build a 1D CNN in PyTorch that takes raw I/Q samples as input and predicts the target's signal type and parameters. Feed it the raw data with minimal preprocessing — the network learns the patterns itself.

**Why:** This is the core AI contribution of the project. The whole point is to let the network work directly on raw I/Q instead of processed features. This is your strongest skill area and is where the project gets exciting.

---

### Step 5 — Compare and Evaluate
Run both methods — the traditional detector and the neural network — on the same RadChar test data. Vary the noise level and see how each method holds up. Plot the results clearly.

**Why:** The comparison is what makes this a real project and not just a homework exercise. You are answering a genuine question: under what conditions does AI beat traditional signal processing, and when does it not? That nuance is what impresses people.

---

### Step 6 — Write It Up and Publish on GitHub
Clean up all notebooks. Write a clear README that explains what the project is, why it matters, what you did, and what you found. Include your best plots. Make it readable to someone who has never heard of radar.

**Why:** A project that is not presented does not exist as far as a portfolio is concerned. The README is your pitch. The plots are your proof. This step turns months of work into something you can share and be proud of.

---

## What a Good Result Looks Like

At the end of this project you will have:
- A solid understanding of radar signal processing fundamentals
- A working implementation of a traditional radar detector
- A trained neural network that operates on raw I/Q data
- A clean comparison between the two approaches across different noise levels
- A well-documented GitHub repository you can show to anyone

You do not need to beat the traditional method definitively. Real research rarely has clean wins. What matters is that you ran the experiment properly, understood what you found, and presented it honestly.
